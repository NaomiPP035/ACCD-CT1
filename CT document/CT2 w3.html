WEEK3 - Archeology of the Futures

My project starts from a very practical frustration: most phone stands today are rigid, angle-limited, and designed for a desk-only lifestyle. I wanted to ask a design fiction question: if our daily life becomes more mobile and less attached to fixed furniture, what kind of support object would feel natural?

I imagined a future society that values minimalism, but not in a "cold and empty" way. In my world map (Big/Small and Black & White/Colorful), minimalism sits in the "Small + Black and White" quadrant. This means people prioritize reduction, low visual noise, and anti-excess consumption. Homes are smaller, objects are fewer, and every product is expected to be multi-functional, soft in behavior, and long-lasting.

At the cultural level, this society treats flexibility as a new form of luxury. Instead of owning many specialized products, people own one adaptive product that can transform by touch. At the social level, maintenance and repair are more important than novelty. Products are judged by how quietly they integrate into life, not by how loudly they show technology. This changes design priorities from "more features" to "fewer but deeper interactions."

Based on that logic, my plausible future phone stand becomes a tentacle-like support tool: no hinge, no hard click-joints, no predefined positions. It behaves like a soft limb. The material is imagined as a liquid-glass polymer: visually close to glass or polished metal, but physically bendable and shape-retaining. So the object can wrap around a handrail, car interior edge, or backpack strap, then hold the phone in place.



Future World

World: Cities are dense, shared, and constantly in motion. People watch, work, and communicate in transit, so support objects must work outside stable tabletop environments.

Culture: "Less but better" is dominant. Visual language favors neutral tones and reduced form, while material intelligence (how a thing behaves) becomes more important than decorative complexity.

Society: Planned obsolescence is criticized. Communities prefer products that can be re-shaped, repaired, and passed on. This creates demand for soft, durable, and reusable objects.

Priorities: Adaptability, portability, emotional calm, and low resource consumption. In this context, my stand is not just an accessory but a response to a social value system.



Making Process

I referenced an octopus-tentacle modeling tutorial workflow and translated it into my own object design. The key method was to build from a curve-driven form first, then iterate material and context instead of jumping directly to final rendering.

Step 1: I made a shape that feels like it was softly stretched in a car interior environment. I tested proportions first: thickness, bend radius, and contact area for phone support. I also started to think about an internal gradient structure (denser core, softer skin) even though it is represented visually at this stage.


Step 2: I built a material that sits between glass and metal in appearance. I tried to balance roughness and specular so it could look premium but still soft in behavior,but no matter how I change the value of the material, I failed to make the ideal material, which should kind of looks like slime. So, I make it looks hard, but behaves soft.

Step 3: I refined the overall shape and adjusted camera angles for usability storytelling. I could not find a suitable car interior model, so I used HDRI environments to simulate reflective context. This helped me evaluate how the material reads under different light situations and whether the object still communicates "support + flexibility" without a full scene model.

Outcome view A: demonstrates how the object can behave as a stable support while maintaining an organic, non-mechanical form.

Outcome view B: emphasizes material identity and the minimal aesthetic direction of the proposed future culture.
